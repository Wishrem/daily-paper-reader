Abstract. Non-autoregressive generation offers a powerful paradigm for iterative refinement, allowing models to recursively critique, erase and regenerate arbitrary subsets of tokens. However, existing non-autoregressive models fail to realize this potential. Masked Diffusion Models (MDMs) suffer from factorization error, causing sample quality to collapse when generating multiple tokens simultaneously. Flow Map Language Models (FMLMs) circumvent this bottleneck via joint sequence transport for excellent few-step generation, but sacrifice the inference-time flexibility of MDMs. We introduce FMLM +, a framework that bridges this gap by equipping FMLM with masking-style noise schedules. While generating the full sequence in a single step, FMLM + simultaneously scores the global consistency of each token a posteriori . We leverage this to introduce Posterior Refinement , a novel inference-time refinement strategy that enables the model to adaptively self-correct its outputs, matching the performance of discrete baselines with 32 × fewer NFEs. Across diverse benchmarks, we demonstrate that FMLM + with Posterior Refinement improves the speed–quality tradeoff over both MDM and FMLM families, providing a scalable foundation for high-fidelity language modeling.

Contemporary language models are predominantly autore-gressive (AR), necessitating L sequential forward passes to generate a sequence of length L [ 1–3 ]. While this paradigm has scaled remarkably well, its inherent sequential bottleneck makes inference slow and memory-bound [ 4, 5]. These inef-ficiencies cascade into downstream applications like inference-time scaling, reasoning, and reinforcement learning, which are inference-bottlenecked, ultimately hindering scalability [3]. Masked diffusion models (MDMs) have emerged as the leading non-autoregressive alternative for language modeling [ 6–8 ], with recent efforts scaling them to billions of parameters [ 9–13 ]. In contrast to fixed causal orderings, MDMs model any-order factorizations of the sequence by training on arbitrary masking patterns. During inference, this permits adaptive unmask-and-remask decoding strategies that commit high-confidence tokens first [ 14 – 16 ]. Such a flexible generative procedure has proven useful in planning-oriented tasks [17]. 1

Complementing discrete formulations, a distinct line of work explores diffusion and flows within continuous representations for non-autoregressive language modeling, spanning fixed embeddings [ 18 –20 ], learned latent spaces [21, 22], and one-hot relaxations [23–25]. Recently, continuous-space approaches have been shown to be competitive with their discrete-diffusion counterparts in language modeling [ 20 , 22 , 26 , 27 ]. In particular, Flow Map Language Models (FMLMs) [ 26 ] train flow maps [ 28 ] over one-hot embeddings in Euclidean space, resolving the inherent factorization error in MDMs [ 29 –31 ] by learning a joint transport from Gaussian noise to one-hot vectors. This allows FMLMs to substantially improve the quality of few-step language generation, including generating text in just a single function evaluation [26]. Beyond fast, parallel inference, non-autoregressive generation offers a more profound theoretical advantage over AR modeling: the capacity for iterative refinement of generated text. While traditional causal language models are fundamentally constrained by a one-way, left-to-right generation, an iterative refinement paradigm allows the model to globally critique, erase, and regenerate arbitrary subsets of tokens to continually improve its output. This capacity directly mirrors human cognition; when composing complex text, humans rarely write perfectly in a single, unyielding left-to-right pass. Instead, we efficiently formulate a rough draft to capture the global context, and then iteratively assess, delete, and rewrite specific parts of text while keeping the overall picture in context [32]. However, existing non-autoregressive paradigms fail to fully realize this potential. While MDMs offer flexible, any-order inference, their quality rapidly degrades when multiple tokens are unmasked simultaneously, rendering few-step generation infeasible [ 26 , 29 –31 ]. On the contrary, FMLMs resolve this factorization error by learning the joint transport from Gaussian noise to one-hot vectors, yet they lack the inference-time flexibility native to MDMs because they evolve all tokens simultaneously. We argue that the respective strengths of these two paradigms constitute the core primitives for effective iterative refinement, which requires both — efficient drafting of coherent multi-token blocks and the flexibility to retain arbitrary token subsets while regenerating the rest. Motivated by this insight, we introduce FMLM +, a unified framework that equips FMLMs with masking-style noise schedules. By coupling single-step sequence generation from FMLMs with flexible conditioning from MDMs, this architecture naturally supports iterative refinement. Crucially, because the entire sequence is transported jointly, our approach yields an a posteriori confidence score for each token conditioned on the full draft . This contrasts sharply with the a priori confidence derived from independent token-wise marginals in MDMs. We leverage this property to develop Posterior Refinement ( PR ) — an iterative refinement strategy that recursively retains high-confidence tokens and regenerates the rest to keep improving sample quality with each additional round of refinement (Figure 1). Subsequently, FMLM + with Posterior Refinement establishes a new state-of-the-art that consistently outperforms both MDM and FMLM baselines across all considered benchmarks with far fewer function evaluations (NFEs). Our contributions are as follows: 1. A unifying framework. We introduce FMLM +, a generalization of FMLMs to masking-style noise schedules that combines the few-step efficiency of FMLMs with the any-order flexibility of MDMs. We further establish a connection between MDMs and FMLM +, providing a direct path to scale FMLM +

leveraging advances in large-scale MDM pre-training [9, 10]. 2. Posterior refinement. We introduce Posterior Refinement , an after-the-fact confidence-based strategy that enables FMLM + to recursively self-correct its output while generating all remaining tokens with 

single function evaluations (Figure 2). We identify how existing a-priori confidence heuristics of MDMs fail on simple toy problems and demonstrate how Posterior Refinement is able to rectify these shortcomings. 3. Extensive evaluation. We demonstrate that FMLM + navigates the speed–quality trade-off more effec-tively than both MDM and FMLM families across four benchmarks: TinyStories [ 33 ], OpenWebText [ 34 ], GSM8K [35], and Sudoku, matching or surpassing the strongest baselines with up to 32 × fewer NFEs. 2Before

## 2 Background 

Let V denote a vocabulary, with |V| = V , and let y = ( yl)Ll=1 ∈ V L be a sequence of length L drawn from a data distribution p(y).

Autoregressive Models. The AR paradigm models the data distribution through a left-to-right factor-ization p(y) = p(y1)p(y2|y1) . . . p (yL|y<L ), and learns the conditional distributions p(yl | y<l ) over tokens given their preceding context [ 36 – 38 ]. Generation proceeds sequentially by sampling one token at a time conditioned on previously generated tokens. 

Masked Diffusion Models. Masked Diffusion Models (MDMs) [ 6 –8] offer an alternative paradigm for discrete generative modeling, parameterizing marginals over clean tokens given a partially masked input. MDMs extend the vocabulary V with an extra [MASK ] token. A monotonic fraction α(t) of clean tokens y is masked to produce yt. The token-wise marginal pθ (yl | yt) is then trained via a classification loss over the masked positions,

Flow Matching and Flow Maps. Flow matching [ 39 –41 ] learns a continuous transport from a simple source distribution (e.g., Gaussian noise) to the target data distribution by regressing the velocity field of a probability flow ODE along a prescribed source–target interpolant. We define x1 = f (y) as the one-hot representation of clean data, and sample x0 ∼ N(0 , I ) as isotropic Gaussian noise of matching shape to construct the linear interpolant in Euclidean space:

Continuous diffusion and flow-based models typically denoise the full sequence in parallel [ 39 , 46 , 47 ]. While effective for unconditional generation, this formulation has no native mechanism for conditioning on clean context. Unlike autoregressive or masked models, where conditioning is a structural primitive, continuous-flow models often require architectural modifications, such as cross-attention or auxiliary guidance models, to incorporate conditional information [ 48 , 49 ]. To address this limitation in flow-based language modeling, we introduce FMLM +, a generalization of FMLMs that natively learns to generate under diverse conditioning patterns (Section 3.1). We show that this allows for flexible decoding strategies at inference time (Section 3.2), such as FMLM- and MDM- like inference, and further unlocks novel iterative refinement schemes (Section 3.3).

Beyond recovering existing inference schemes as in Section 3.2, we show that FMLM + enables a novel class of after-the-fact refinement strategies native to neither the MDM nor the FMLM paradigms. During inference, FMLM + steps along the flow trajectory to simultaneously decode all positions. At the final integration step, because the model operates on a nearly complete sequence, the model implicitly evaluates the conditional probability of each token given the rest of the generated text. Interpretation. An ideal FMLM would per-fectly sample from its training distribution, al-ways producing one-hot vectors. In practice, how-ever, the model outputs categorical distributions that are not strictly one-hot. To resolve this, Lee et al. [26] project each token to the nearest ver-tex of the probability simplex via the argmax operator. We interpret the rounding error of this projection as a proxy for the model’s confidence. This interpretation aligns with our empirical ob-servations in Sudoku, where we find that incorrect tokens consistently exhibit high rounding error (Figure 2). Concretely, this rounding error can be quanti-fied using either the ℓ1 distance or the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the generated distribution and the target one-hot distribution. Why a-priori confidence fails. Because both the MDM denoiser pθ and the FMLM denoiser δ0,0 predict token-wise marginals, independently sampling high-confidence tokens from these marginals cannot circumvent factorization error. While this heuristic performs well on unimodal target distributions such as unique Sudoku solutions, it catastrophically fails on multi-modal distributions (Table 6). We identify that MDMs, and equivalently δ0,0, conflate aleatoric uncertainty, the inherent randomness of the data distribution, with epistemic uncertainty, the model’s internal confidence [ 54 ]. We hypothesize that by failing to perfectly learn the joint transport from Gaussian noise to clean one-hot data, the FMLM trajectory is able to surface the true epistemic uncertainty of the model through errors in its predictions (Figure 5). 74 Experiments 

We evaluate FMLM + on four benchmarks: TinyStories [ 33 ] and OpenWebText [ 34 ] for unconditional language generation, TinyGSM [ 55 ] for conditional reasoning, and Sudoku for structured constraint satisfaction. Following Kim et al. [17] , we train on code solutions from the synthetic TinyGSM corpus and evaluate zero-shot on the GSM8K [ 35 ] test split. We adopt a DiT [ 50 ] backbone for all baselines; further details on model architecture and datasets are provided in Appendix B.

For the MDM-distillation and -initialization experiments (Section 4.2), we additionally train an MDLM model [ 8] with the same architecture to act as a teacher and an initialization checkpoint respectively. Appendix B.3 specifies all task-specific training hyperparameters.

At t= 0 (pure Gaussian noise), FMLM + activations remain highly aligned with the teacher’s [MASK ] activations. Thus, we conclude that the first adaLN layer maps the Gaussian-noise input onto the same representation the teacher produced for [MASK ] tokens, presenting downstream layers with an in-distribution input and preserving the pretrained knowledge. In Table 12, we further compute the per-token cosine similarity between each FMLM + input embedding and its MDM-init counterpart. Every token embedding stays aligned with its initialization after training, indicating that FMLM + adapts the embed-ding layer to be robust to Gaussian noise corruptions. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We introduce FMLM +, a generalization of flow map language models that equips continuous-flow language modeling with the any-order inference flexibility of masked diffusion, paving the way towards effective iterative refinement. We further introduce Posterior Refinement , a novel, after-the-fact refinement strategy that re-noises and regenerates low-confidence tokens to self-correct in parallel. Empirically, FMLM + improves the speed-quality tradeoff over both MDM and FMLM families across unconditional and conditional generation settings, matching or surpassing the strongest baselines with up to 32 × fewer function evaluations. Finally, we show that the masked diffusion objective is equivalent to a boundary case of the FMLM + training objective, and use this correspondence to derive two practical mechanisms based on distillation and transfer learning for accelerating FMLM + training using pretrained MDMs. 

Future directions. The correspondence between MDM and FMLM + offers a path to scale FMLM + to the billion-parameter regime using the growing pool of large pretrained masked diffusion models (LLaDA-8B [ 9], Dream [ 10 ], and others). We also believe that the conditional nature of FMLM + can be leveraged to improve generation quality using techniques such as classifier-free guidance [ 58 ]. Finally, Posterior Refinement exposes a form of inference-time scaling native to FMLM +, and we expect novel test-time strategies can be designed around such posterior-confidence signals. We leave a systematic study of these directions to future work. 9Limitations. Methodologically, we follow the progressive self-distillation objective from Lee et al. [26] and do not explore alternatives such as the Lagrangian, Eulerian and Mean Flow variants [ 28 , 42 ]. We conduct our experiments on a small scale on largely synthetic and small-sized datasets. Thus, the scalability of our approach needs a more broad study with evaluation on downstream tasks. While our current formulation of PR holds committed tokens fixed, alternative formulations can be explored to allow FMLM + to rectify previous errors. Finally, we note that training from scratch is suboptimal compared to initializing with MDM weights, even at convergence. This implies that there is room to improve FMLM + training with better model architecture and low-variance loss functions.

## B Setup 

We document the configurations used to produce all reported results (summarized in Appendix B.4). Following prior work [ 8, 26 ], we adopt a DiT [ 50 ] backbone for FMLM +. All experiments are run on 4× NVIDIA H100 GPUs, in bfloat16 mixed precision, with AdamW ( β1= 0.9, β2= 0.999 , ϵ=10 −8), no weight decay, and gradient-norm clipping at 1.0. Learning rates follow a 2.5k-step linear warmup to a constant peak. We evaluate using exponential moving average (EMA, decay 0.9999 ) parameters. 

B.1 Datasets 

TinyStories (unconditional generation). We train on TinyStories [ 33 ], a corpus of GPT-3.5/4-generated short stories restricted to a limited vocabulary. We tokenize with the GPT-2 byte-pair tokenizer and pack examples into length-128 sequences with <EOS> -insertion at sequence boundaries. We report generative perplexity under GPT-2 Large following [ 8, 26 , 29 ], alongside per-sample token entropy as a check against entropy collapse. Results appear in Table 7. 14 OpenWebText (unconditional generation). We also evaluate on OpenWebText [ 34 ], an open reproduc-tion of the WebText corpus used to train GPT-2. We tokenize with the GPT-2 byte-pair tokenizer and pack documents into length-1024 sequences with <EOS> -insertion at document boundaries. As with TinyStories, we report generative perplexity under GPT-2 Large [ 8, 26 , 29 ], alongside per-sample token entropy as a check against entropy collapse. Results appear in Table 7. 

TinyGSM / GSM8K (conditional reasoning). We follow the exact protocol of S-FLM [ 59 ] so that our numbers are directly comparable to its baseline suite (MDLM, Duo, CANDI, FLM, S-FLM). We train on TinyGSM [ 55 ], a synthetic corpus of 12.3M grade-school math word problems generated to imitate the GSM8K distribution. We tokenize with the SmolLM-135M tokenizer and pack each ⟨question , Python solution ⟩ pair to length 512 , with loss supervised only on the solution tokens. Models are evaluated zero-shot on the GSM8K test split by sampling a Python solution conditioned on the prompt and grading by execution accuracy. 

Sudoku (conditional). Following S-FLM [ 59 ], each puzzle is encoded as a length-180 sequence over a 

12 -token vocabulary, with loss supervised only on the solution positions. Models are evaluated on the rate at which they produce a valid completion of the clue set across Easy/Medium/Hard splits. Results appear in Table 7. 

B.2 Architecture 

All models use the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) backbone [ 50 ] with adaLN time conditioning. Following Lee et al. [26] , FMLM + uses a double time embedding ( s and t are both injected through adaLN) so the same transformer can serve as the two-time denoiser at training and as a single-time denoiser at s=t. The MDM baselines and the MDM teachers used for distillation/initialization use the standard single-time DiT. The exact per-task configurations are listed in Appendix B.4; for the warm-start and distillation variants, FMLM +

reuses the architecture of the corresponding MDM so that initialization is a parameter-identity copy.
